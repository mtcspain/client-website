What Is a “Hombre Bueno” in Spanish Labour Conciliation?

1 min readLabour

If you're caught in a workplace dispute in Spain and want to avoid a drawn-out court battle, there's a lesser-known option worth understanding: bringing in a "hombre bueno."

The term literally means "good man," and in Spanish labour law it refers to a neutral, trusted third party who can step into a conciliation process between a worker and an employer, usually before the case reaches court.

What do they actually do

A hombre bueno acts as a voluntary mediator. They listen to both sides and offer fair, balanced proposals to help the worker and employer reach an agreement. This is typically someone respected and impartial, with real experience in labour matters.

Are they in charge of the outcome

No. Whatever they recommend isn't legally binding on its own. But if both sides accept the proposal, the resulting agreement carries full legal validity, and the case never needs to go to court.

When does this come into play

Using a hombre bueno is entirely optional, and both sides have to agree to it. It tends to work well when the parties want to resolve things in a more human, informal, and constructive way, rather than through a formal legal fight.

If you're dealing with a workplace conflict in Spain, this route can be worth considering. It's not for every case, but where both sides are open to it, it can lead to a fair resolution without the cost and stress of litigation.
