Kids, WhatsApp & Risk

2 min readFamily Law

Your child has a smartphone, and probably WhatsApp. Maybe you have already seen a message that worried you, or a conversation with someone you did not recognise. If so, you are not overreacting. Apps like WhatsApp are part of everyday life for kids now, but they can quickly expose them to situations they are not ready to handle.

This is not just a parenting question. Under Spanish law, parents have a duty to supervise their children, and that includes their digital activity. Waiting too long to act can lead to real risks, from inappropriate conversations to contact with strangers.

Act early, and act together

If both parents are involved in the child's life, work from the same playbook. Mixed messages between parents weaken any boundary you try to set. Consistency is what makes it stick.

Don't just ban it, manage it

Taking WhatsApp away is not always the right answer. Match your response to the problem:

  • A minor issue calls for supervision, clear rules, and regular checking in.
  • A serious concern calls for restricting or removing the app for a while.

The goal here is protection, not punishment.

Have the conversation

Children need to hear the limits in plain terms, something like: "Some things online can lead to serious problems, even if you don't realise it at the time." Keep the tone calm and firm, and keep the focus on safety rather than blame.

Some boundaries worth setting for any child with a smartphone:

  • No talking to strangers.
  • No sharing photos or personal details.
  • Regular check-ins from a parent.

These are not just good parenting habits. They are legally justified under your parental responsibility.

When to get help

Get advice if you see any of the following:

  • Contact from an adult.
  • Sexual or otherwise inappropriate content.
  • Pressure, threats, or manipulation.

This is about guidance and prevention, not overreacting. The earlier parents step in, the safer their children will be.
