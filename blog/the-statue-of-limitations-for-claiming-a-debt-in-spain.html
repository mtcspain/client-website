The Statue of Limitations for Claiming a Debt in Spain

2 min readCivil Law & Disputes

If someone owes you money in Spain, or you think an old debt of yours might no longer be enforceable, timing matters. Debts do not stay claimable forever. Spanish law puts a time limit on how long a creditor has to take action, and once that window closes, the debt can no longer be pursued.

This time limit is often called a statute of limitations, or in civil law terms a prescriptive period. It simply means the law sets a maximum time after which legal proceedings for a debt can no longer be started.

How long you have

For most ordinary debts, Spanish law gives you 5 years from the date the debt was due, counted from date to date. But not every debt follows this general rule. Several types of debt have their own shorter or longer time limits:

  • Non-contractual liability (for example, compensation for harm caused outside a contract): 1 year
  • Payments owed to notaries, lawyers, and teachers: 3 years
  • Mortgage debts: 20 years
  • Unpaid utility bills (telephone, water, electricity, etc.): 3 years
  • Unpaid rent: 5 years
  • Unpaid alimony or maintenance payments: 5 years
  • Debts to Social Security: 4 years
  • Debts to the Tax Agency: 4 years

What this means for you

If you are owed money, do not let the clock run out. Once the relevant period passes, you lose the right to claim the debt through the courts.

The good news is that the clock can be reset. Sending the debtor a reliable, formal notice, ideally one issued by a lawyer, interrupts the prescriptive period. That means the countdown starts again from scratch, giving you a fresh window to act.

If you are unsure how much time you have left on a specific debt, or you want to make sure a notice is sent in a way that properly interrupts the limitation period, it is worth getting this checked before the deadline passes.
