Protecting Privacy in the Digital Age: The Right to Be Forgotten on the Internet

1 min readCivil Law & Disputes

Imagine an old news story, a mistake from years ago, or an outdated post about you still shows up whenever someone searches your name. It is not fair, and in the digital world it can quietly damage your reputation. This is exactly what the right to be forgotten on the internet is designed to address.

The right to be forgotten is your right to control the disclosure of personal information online that is no longer relevant or that causes you disproportionate harm. In practice, it means you can ask for content to be removed, or ask search engines and social media platforms to de-index it so it no longer appears in searches.

This right matters most in cases of outdated information, factual errors, defamation, or breaches of privacy. It lets you take back control of your own information, so that something from your past, or something simply irrelevant, does not follow you around and cause harm indefinitely.

At the same time, this right is not absolute. It has to be balanced against freedom of expression, and finding that balance, protecting someone's privacy without unfairly restricting the free flow of information, is not always straightforward.

In short, the right to be forgotten is a real legal tool to protect your privacy and reputation online. It exists so your past does not permanently define you, and so you can move forward even in an increasingly complex digital world.
