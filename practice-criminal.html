Common questions
I have been called in for a police statement. Do I need a lawyer? +

Yes, always. Under Spanish law you have the right to a lawyer present for any police statement, whether you are a witness or a suspect. What you say at this stage can shape the whole case, so it matters that someone is there who understands both the law and what you are actually saying.

What happens after I am charged? +

The case first goes through an investigation phase before a Juzgado de Instrucción, where evidence is gathered. Depending on the severity of the charge, it then moves either to a Juzgado de lo Penal for less serious offences or to the Audiencia Provincial for more serious ones, where the actual trial takes place.

Can a case be appealed if I am found guilty? +

Yes, in most cases. Depending on where the original judgment was issued, an appeal (recurso de apelación) goes to a higher court, and in some circumstances a further appeal can reach the Tribunal Supremo. Appeal deadlines are strict, so this needs to be assessed quickly after a judgment.

Do I have to attend court in person if I live abroad? +

It depends on the stage and the seriousness of the case. Some hearings can proceed with legal representation alone, but a criminal trial where you are the accused generally requires your presence. We tell you upfront exactly which hearings need you there in person.

What is a European Arrest Warrant and what should I do if I receive one? +

A European Arrest Warrant is issued by one EU country asking another to arrest and surrender a person for prosecution or to serve a sentence. If you are notified of one, you should get legal representation immediately, since there are strict time limits and specific grounds on which surrender can be challenged.
